300€ DIY Electric Mountain Bike Project v1

Antoine Pirrone and Maxime Palesi

I have been wanting to build a rather powerful (>2000W) electric mountain bike for a while now, but the high price of powerful conversion kits was always stopping me (often more than 500€ wih the batteries for 2000W or more).

While researching other solutions online I found that video (in french), where a custom ~2000W bike is made for under 200€ (not including the bike). That convinced me investigate more, and to actually start the project.

When talking about it with my cousin, he got interested and we decided to do it together, splitting the costs. Our initial goal is to spend a maximum of 300€ in total (bike included).


The specs we would like to achieve are the following :

  • Enough torque to start from standstill
  • About 20kms of range
  • About 30-40 km/h max speed (we don't really need to go fast)

So we started listing and sourcing everything we needed :

  • A used mountain bike, with front and rear suspensions and disk brakes
  • A sensored brushless DC electric motor producing between 2000 and 3000 Watts
  • A controller capable of handling the current pulled by the motor
  • A battery pack capable of delivering enough current, and of sufficient capacity to allow the desired range
  • A belt for transmission
  • A large pulley to increase torque
  • A twist throttle
  • Aluminum sheets to be cnc machined to make mounting pieces
  • Various laying around stuff (cables, connectors etc)

We will make a 8s4p battery pack with our 4s 2800mah 30c lipos. One 4s lipo battery gives us about 16.5V fully charged, so putting 2 of them in series gives us about 33V. Putting 4 of these blocks in parralel, we get a battery pack with the following specifications :

  • 33V nominal tension
  • (8.4Ah/268.8Wh) capacity
  • 252A peak current
  • 8064 peak power

Which should be more than enough, since our motor should have the following specs :

  • 24V working tension
  • 125A peak current
  • 3000W max power

We can use an estimation of 12.5Wh/km (found here and here) to calculate an estimated range of about 21kms
